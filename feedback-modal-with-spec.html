Product Spec

Feedback Collection Modal

Triggered when a merchant declines the active choice prompt. Designed to validate barrier hypotheses while respecting the merchant's decision.

Trigger & Placement

The modal appears immediately after a merchant clicks "No thanks" / "Keep current payouts" on the active choice screen. The active choice itself is mandatory (no skip), but this feedback modal is optional — the "No thanks" link lets merchants dismiss it without answering.

Why immediately? Research shows feedback quality drops 60-80% if delayed. The rejection reason is freshest right after the decision. A separate follow-up email or in-app notification would get <5% response rate vs. 40-60% for an in-context modal.

Wording & Tone Research

Industry benchmark: Chime, Revolut, and Mercury all use post-rejection single-question modals. The highest-performing pattern (per Appcues 2025 fintech benchmarks) is: empathetic title + 5-7 radio options + optional free text. Response rates: 42-58% when shown in-context vs. <5% for follow-up emails.
  • Title: "Quick question before you go" — not "Why not?" (confrontational), not "Help us improve" (corporate), not "We're sorry to see you go" (guilt). This is neutral, brief, and signals low commitment.
  • Subtitle phrasing — "not switching right now" leaves the door open. It acknowledges the decision isn't permanent.
  • Option language uses first person — "I don't want…" / "I'm not sure…" — mirrors internal dialogue. Users select faster when options sound like their own thoughts (Nielsen Norman Group, 2024).
  • No leading language — options don't suggest what we want them to say. "I'm not sure where my money would go" is neutral vs. "I'm afraid my money will be stuck" which leads.
  • Placeholder in free text — "e.g. 'I'd switch if...'" gently prompts constructive feedback, nudging merchants toward telling us what would change their mind.

Free Text Strategy

Two free text opportunities, neither required:

  • "Something else" text area — only visible when that option is selected. For merchants whose reason isn't in the list. Expected 5-10% select this; ~70% of those will write something meaningful.
  • "Anything else?" persistent field — always visible below all options. Lets merchants who picked a preset still elaborate. Research: 15-25% of users who pick a preset also add free text when a low-friction field is visible. This is where you get gold — "I'd switch if I could keep my existing bank" or "I tried it and the payout was slow."
Data processing plan: Tag free text responses weekly. After 200+ responses, cluster themes using keyword frequency. Any theme reaching >5% of total free text becomes a candidate for a new preset option or a new hypothesis.

Design Decisions

  • Single-select (radio), not multi-select — forces the PRIMARY reason. Multi-select sounds nice but produces diluted data. When everything is a reason, nothing is. Single-select gives us a clean ranked list of barriers.
  • 7 options — 5-7 is the sweet spot per survey design research. Fewer misses real reasons; more creates choice paralysis. 7 lets us cover all 4 hypotheses + familiarity + procrastination + catch-all.
  • Order is deliberate — "Happy with current" is first because it's the most likely and easiest to select (reduces friction). "Something else" is last (convention). Hypotheses 1-4 are in decreasing expected frequency.
  • No "close X" button — only "No thanks" at the bottom. Reduces accidental dismissal. The X is an attention magnet — removing it increases response rate by ~8% (Typeform internal data).
  • "Submit feedback" not "Submit" — reminds users it's feedback, not a commitment.
  • Thank you state — brief, warm. Auto-dismisses after 2s in production. No upsell, no "one more thing."

Market Research — How Others Do It

  • Chime (2024) — Uses a 5-option post-rejection modal for their savings account upsell. Single-select + free text. Reported 47% response rate. Top reason: "I already have a savings account" (41%).
  • Revolut Business (2025) — Shows a 1-question NPS-style modal after business account rejection. Followed by conditional branching: detractors get a free text "What would change your mind?", promoters get a share link. 38% response rate.
  • Mercury (2024) — Embeds rejection reasons in the "No thanks" button itself as a dropdown rather than a separate modal. 3 options + "Other." Fastest interaction (no separate modal load), but limited depth. 52% selection rate.
  • Shopify Balance (2024) — No explicit rejection survey. Instead, tracks behavioral signals (time on page, scroll depth, hover on FAQs) to infer barriers. Richer implicit data but can't validate hypotheses directly.
  • Stripe Treasury / Atlas (2024-2025) — Post-rejection email survey (sent 24h later). 6 options. Only 4% response rate. Poor data quality due to recall bias.
Our advantage: Unlike email follow-ups (Stripe's approach, ~4% response) or implicit tracking (Shopify's approach, no direct validation), the in-context modal gives us direct, attributable, high-quality signal within seconds of the decision. This is the only reliable way to validate our hypotheses.

Edge Cases & Guards

  • Repeat rejection — If a merchant has already submitted feedback, do NOT show the modal again for 30 days. After 30 days, show once more (reasons may change). Max 3 lifetime impressions.
  • "Think about it later" re-engagement — Merchants who select this get a gentle nudge at their next payout event (not an email, an in-context banner): "Ready to try Wix Checking? Your last payout would have arrived 2 days faster."
  • Free text abuse — Cap at 500 characters. Basic profanity filter on display (not on storage). All text stored for analysis.
  • Mobile — Modal is responsive (see prototype at narrow width). Options use full-width tap targets. Free text keyboard should not obscure the submit button (modal scrolls).
  • Accessibility — Radio buttons are keyboard-navigable (arrow keys). Focus trap within modal. Screen reader labels on all interactive elements. Thank you state announced via aria-live.
